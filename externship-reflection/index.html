Reflection Guide · Externship Debrief

What Did You Bring Back?

Slow down, make sense of what you saw, and find the ideas worth bringing to your students.

At a Glance

Suggested Time
45–60 minutes
Format
Individual writing, then group discussion
Materials
This guide + something to write with
The Four Steps
01 · Unload 02 · Zoom In 03 · Name It 04 · Build the Bridge

You just experienced something real. This guide helps you slow down, make sense of what you saw, and find the ideas worth bringing to your students.

Each step asks you to write a little, then talk with your group. Don't rush — the most useful ideas usually surface in the conversation.

Tip — You'll need this guide and something to write with.
01
Step One

Unload

Before you analyze anything, just get it all out. Write quickly and don't edit yourself. You're not looking for meaning yet — just capturing what's still alive from the externship.

Each person writes their own answers:
  • What are you still thinking about from the externship? List everything — moments, images, conversations, details that stuck.
  • What surprised you?
  • What made you uncomfortable, confused, or curious?
Your notes
Tip — When everyone's done, take 2 minutes each to read your list out loud. Don't comment yet — just listen.
02
Step Two

Zoom In

Look back at what you wrote. One or two things probably stand out — a moment you keep returning to. That's where the real learning tends to hide.

Each person picks ONE moment from the externship and describes it as specifically as possible:
  • What exactly was happening? Who was there, what were they doing?
  • What did it make you think or feel in the moment?
  • Why do you think it's sticking with you?
Describe your moment
Then share your moment with the group. As each person shares, listen for: what does this moment reveal about how this workplace actually operates?
What patterns did you notice across everyone's moments?
03
Step Three

Name It

This is the most important step. You're moving from what you saw to what it means — from story to concept.

A concept is the big idea underneath the moment. For example: you watched workers run a safety checklist before getting on the roof. The concept isn't "safety checklists" — it's something deeper, like building safety into a process so it becomes automatic, not optional.

Tip — Try finishing this sentence: "The real thing we saw was not ___ but ___." That second blank is usually your concept.
Work through these questions as a group:
  • Look at your moments from Step 2. What's the concept underneath each one?
  • What problem were people solving — not just on the surface, but at a deeper level?
  • What did "doing this work well" actually look like? What did quality mean here?
  • If you had to name the one most important idea this workplace runs on, what would it be?
The two or three concepts or big ideas we're walking away with
04
Step Four

Build the Bridge

Now you have something real. The question is what to do with it.

Take the concepts from Step 3 and start imagining how they could live in your classroom. You don't need a finished plan — you need a spark.

For each concept you named, ask:
  • Where does this concept already show up in what we teach — even loosely?
  • What would it look like if students had to actually grapple with this problem?
  • Could this anchor a project? A driving question? Even just one lesson?
  • Is there someone from this site who could come talk to our students?
Our best ideas for bringing this into the classroom
Before you leave

Each person completes this out loud and writes it down:

"The concept I most want to bring to students is ___, and my next step is ___ by ___."
