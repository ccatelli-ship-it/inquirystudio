Designing Driving Questions

Turn your strongest links into one question that can drive a project.

This method helps teacher teams turn the links they already found into a single driving question for project-based learning. You begin with the link statements from Looking for Links, unpack the learning and the spark hiding inside each one, draft many possible questions, test them against quality criteria, and use peer critique to sharpen a launch-ready version.

A strong driving question does more than introduce a project — it gives students a reason to inquire, build knowledge, practice skills, and create something meaningful. The temptation is to start with a product or activity. Resist it. Your link statements already point at the core learning and the reason it matters; this activity widens that spark into a question worth investigating. The process is grounded in three filters: viable (it requires the targeted learning), feasible (you can realistically pursue it), and desirable (students actually want to investigate it).

Start From Your Links

You are not starting from a blank page. Each link statement already contains the raw material for a driving question — you just have to unpack it.

From Link Statement → Driving Question

A Looking for Links statement has three moving parts. Each one feeds a different part of your driving question. Read your statement, then pull it apart like this:

We see a possible link between Biology and Patient Care through vital signs because students could use data to make decisions about health and wellness.

Carry your COSI target across too. It sets the question's ambition: Overlapping teams write a question anchored in the shared skill or concept; Shared teams write one question that genuinely needs both courses to answer; Immersed teams write a question broad enough to thread a pathway, partner, or work-based experience over time.

What Makes a Driving Question Work?

Use these before, during, and after drafting. A question does not need to be perfect at first — strong enough to launch inquiry and clear enough to guide design.

Desirable

Students care because the question feels relevant, urgent, interesting, or connected to their lives, community, future, or identity.

Feasible

Students and teachers can realistically investigate and respond with the time, resources, tools, and support available.

Viable

Answering it requires students to learn the targeted standards, content, skills, and competencies — your link's shared idea.

Open-ended

There is no single right answer. The question requires inquiry, reasoning, judgment, and evidence.

6As-minded

It creates room for authenticity, academic rigor, applied learning, adult/world connections, active exploration, or assessment.

Steps

01

Unpack your link statements. Put your 2–3 link statements from Looking for Links on the table. For each one, separate it into three columns: the essential learning (the "through ___" shared skill or concept), the authentic spark (the "because students could ___" outcome), and the disciplines (the two courses). If you are an Overlapping team and skipped the interest and real-world lists, do a quick brainstorm of those now.

Ask: If students answered a question built on this link well, what would they have to learn? What would they have to practice, explain, create, or defend?
02

Name the authentic charge. Grow the "students could ___" spark into a real-world problem, decision, product, tension, audience, or role that gives the learning a reason to exist beyond school.

Prompt: This learning matters because people use it to ______, decide ______, improve ______, understand ______, or create ______.
03

Draft for quantity before quality. Set a 5-minute timer. Individually or as a team, draft as many possible driving questions from your link as you can — aim for 15–25. Do not judge yet.

Push for variety: one practical, one provocative, one community-connected, one student-interest driven, one partner-connected, one philosophical, and one slightly wild version.

04

Sort with the three-circle test. Place your drafts into three piles — strongest, maybe, and not yet — using the filters.

Desirable: Would students care?  Feasible: Can we pull this off?  Viable: Does it require the learning from your link? Keep only questions with some strength in all three.
05

Use AI as a thought partner. Feed it the context first: disciplines, essential learning, student interests, real-world problem, authentic audience, and the product or performance you imagine. Then use AI for four moves — ideation for starting points, refinement to improve wording, alignment to check against PBL criteria, and collaboration to keep pushing toward better questions.

06

Select one draft and write the rationale. Choose the question with the strongest mix of student interest, authentic purpose, and learning alignment. Write a 2–3 sentence rationale: Why might students care? What core learning will it require? What could students create, decide, investigate, or present in response?

07

Run the critique protocol. In small groups, tune each question with a tight timed round.

2 min share the question, intended standards, and a short unit description · 2 min clarifying questions (to understand only) · 2 min warm feedback (name strengths) · 2 min cool feedback (name what to reconsider).
08

Revise and lock the launch version. Use the feedback to make the question clearer, sharper, more open-ended, more student-facing, or more authentic. End with a launch-ready draft. It can still evolve, but it should be strong enough to guide project design.

A Useful Question Frame

How can we, as [person or entity], do [action or challenge] so that [audience or purpose]?

From the link above → e.g., How can we, as student health advocates, use vital-sign data to help people in our community make better decisions about their health?

Framing words
How can we…? How might we…? How should we…? What would happen if…? To what extent…?
Person or entity
Students, community members, designers, analysts, historians, health advocates, city planners, engineers, chefs, etc.
Action or challenge
Design, build, create, investigate, decide, solve, propose, explain, improve, test, communicate.
Audience or purpose
For a public audience, partner, school, client, community need, real-world problem, or meaningful student purpose.

Two Common Missteps

Misstep 1 · Task, not learning

The question is only about making the website, poster, or presentation. Students may build the product but miss the deeper purpose — and the link's essential learning.

Misstep 2 · Fails the "who cares?" test

The question is viable and feasible, but not desirable. Students can answer it and it covers standards, but nothing makes them want to.

Feedback Card · Pressure-Test the Question

Use during peer critique or team tuning. The purpose is to improve the question, not defend the first draft.

Open-ended

Is there more than one possible answer? Does it require inquiry, reasoning, and judgment?

Provocative

Would students be curious enough to begin? Does it feel relevant, urgent, surprising, or connected to their world?

Linked to learning

To answer it well, do students need the targeted knowledge, skills, standards, and competencies from your link?

Authentic

Does it connect to a real problem, audience, role, product, issue, decision, or purpose?

Clear

Could students explain what the question is asking without needing a teacher translation?

Try this AI prompt

"We are designing a PBL unit for ______. Students need to learn ______ and be able to ______. Possible real-world connections include ______. Generate 10 open-ended driving questions that are desirable, feasible, viable, and connected to an authentic audience or purpose."

Optional AI revision prompt

"Here is our driving question and rationale. Act as a PBL coach. Give us three revisions: one more student-friendly, one more authentic to the world of work, and one more open-ended. Explain the trade-off in each version."

Team Deliverable
  • One launch-ready driving question draft, traceable back to one of your link statements
  • A short rationale explaining why the question is desirable, feasible, and viable
  • A list of the core standards, skills, or learning outcomes the question will require
  • Notes from warm and cool feedback
  • A revised version that is clearer, more authentic, and more inquiry-driven

This driving question becomes the anchor for the next move: designing the product, assessment, and project arc.
