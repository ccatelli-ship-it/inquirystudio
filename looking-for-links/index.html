Looking for Links

Turn course mapping into possible integration linkages without forcing a project too early.

Stats

Suggested Time
35–50 minutes
Group Size
Integrated teacher teams
Use When
After teams have completed Curriculum Mapping for Integration
COSI Fit
Overlapping, Shared, or Immersed teams
Output
A link bank of possible shared skills, concepts, questions, problems, products, or pathway threads
The Arc
ScanLinkChoose

This activity helps teacher teams look across their course maps and begin spotting possible links for integration. Teams are not designing the full project yet — they are looking for promising patterns: shared skills, related concepts, student interests, real-world problems, community challenges, or products that could eventually become an integrated learning experience.

Curriculum mapping helps teachers understand what each course is trying to teach. Looking for Links is the next move: teams scan those maps to find where integration might naturally emerge. The activity shifts with the team's COSI target — teams aiming for Overlapping focus on shared skills and concepts; teams aiming for Shared or Immersed add what students are interested in and what real-world problems, challenges, or products could make the work more authentic. The goal is to generate linkages, not lock into a final design. The best links should feel promising, useful, and worth exploring further.

COSI Pathway for This Activity

What to look for — and what you do not need yet — depends on your target.

Overlapping Look for

Shared skills, concepts, practices, tools, language, or standards across courses.

Not yet

Student interest lists or real-world product/problem lists.

Shared Look for

A possible shared question, product, challenge, or project moment that connects courses.

Not yet

A full project plan, final assessment, or completed timeline.

Immersed Look for

A broader pathway thread that could connect courses, partners, WBL, projects, or outcomes over time.

Not yet

A fully built pathway sequence.

Steps

01

Start with the course matrix. Return to the course matrix your team built during Curriculum Mapping for Integration. Each course should already include two lists: (1) what students should know/understand and (2) what students should be able to do. Take two quiet minutes to re-read the full matrix before discussing.

02

Name the team's integration target. Confirm whether your team is aiming for Overlapping, Shared, or Immersed — this determines what kind of links you are looking for. If the team is still at Contextualized, pause here and use a lighter activity such as Relevance Rewrite or World-of-Work Anchor Scan first.

03

If Overlapping
Look for shared ingredients. Scan the course matrix for repeated or related skills, concepts, practices, tools, language, or habits of mind. Mark anything students could practice or transfer across more than one course — evidence, critique, measurement, patient communication, data analysis, design process, safety, collaboration, or technical vocabulary.

04

If Shared or Immersed
Add student interest. Create a new list titled Student Interest. Brainstorm topics, questions, activities, tools, issues, identities, communities, media, trends, or experiences students may care about. Use what you know from student conversations, feedback, developmental trends, and classroom observation. Write quickly; do not judge ideas yet.

05

If Shared or Immersed
Add real-world possibilities. Create a second list titled Real-World Problems, Challenges, or Products. Look for inspiration in your community, current events, real workplace needs, externship experiences, philosophical questions, historical events, natural phenomena, controversial issues, or opportunities to design, plan, produce, or create something.

06

Build possible links. Use the course matrix and the new lists to create possible link statements through three lenses: Link — two courses clearly connect around the same skill, idea, or task; Mirror — two courses explore parallel processes, tensions, or problems; Empathize — student interests or real-world needs reveal a meaningful reason to connect the learning.

07

Ask clarifying questions before choosing. Do one round of clarifying questions. Each teacher explains the links they see; teammates ask: "What would students need to understand first?" "Where does this already show up in your course?" "What would make this meaningful for students?" "What might be hard to coordinate?"

08

Select 2–3 promising links. Choose two or three links worth exploring in a future design activity — without fully designing them yet. Prioritize links that are authentic, useful for student learning, connected to more than one course, and possible within the team's current capacity.

09

Name the links worth chasing. For each link you chose, capture it as a single clear statement using the frame below. Then read them aloud as a team. These aren't commitments — they're the sparks. Post them where everyone can see them, and you'll walk out of the room knowing exactly where your next design conversation begins.

Link Statement Frame

We see a possible link between [course] and [course] through [shared skill, concept, or idea] because students could [meaningful outcome].

e.g., We see a possible link between Biology and Patient Care through vital signs because students could use data to make decisions about health and wellness.

Team Deliverable
  • A reviewed course matrix from the previous activity
  • For Overlapping teams: a list of shared skills, concepts, tools, or language
  • For Shared or Immersed teams: student interest and real-world possibility lists
  • A set of possible links generated through the Link / Mirror / Empathize lenses
  • Two or three link statements to revisit during the next design activity

Based on the Looking for Links activity guide and the COSI integration model.
