Teaching Values Activity

Identify the values that shape who you are — and who you want to be — as an educator.

Stats

Suggested Time
25–35 minutes
Grouping
Individual + teacher team
Output
3–5 teaching values + classroom reflection
The Arc
NameReflectApply

Teaching Values helps educators name the beliefs that guide their classroom choices, relationships, and learning design. Teachers begin individually, narrow a broad values list, and then use those values to reflect on what is already visible in their practice and what needs to be more intentionally brought forward.

Every educator has a professional identity that shapes why and how they teach. This activity makes that identity more explicit. By moving from a broad list of teaching value words to a small set of core values, teachers can better understand what matters most in their classroom, how those values show up with students, and how those values can guide project design, classroom culture, and belonging-centered practice. The activity also invites teachers to look for alignment and gaps: Where are my values already alive in the classroom? Where are they not showing up the way I want them to?

Steps

01

Scan the values list. Individually review the teaching values list. Highlight every word that stands out because it reflects your teaching style, your philosophy, your goals as an educator, or the kinds of student interactions you want to create.

02

Narrow to ten. Review your highlighted words. Using a second color, mark, or symbol, narrow your list to ten values that feel most connected to who you are as a teacher. Consider how students, families, colleagues, and administrators might experience these values in your practice.

03

Select your core teaching values. From your list of ten, select your strongest values. Choose five values if you are working in a team of two, or three values if you are working in a team of three or more.

04

Write your values clearly. Record your final teaching values somewhere visible. Use exact words or short phrases that feel useful to you, not just impressive or generic.

05

Connect values to classroom evidence. For each value, name one way it already shows up in your classroom. Be specific: routines, questions, student roles, feedback practices, norms, assignments, or relationship moves.

06

Identify gaps or tensions. Discuss which values are not showing up as strongly as you want. Name what gets in the way — time, habits, systems, confidence, curriculum, expectations, or adult culture.

07

Apply the values to your project. Choose one value you want to make more visible in your project or classroom design. Name one concrete move that would help students experience that value through the learning, not just hear adults talk about it.

Team Discussion

How do your teaching values show up in your classroom? Which values are not showing up the way you want them to? How can these values shape the project you are designing?
