About this presentation

In 2023, following a fatal stabbing at a Sonoma County high school, SCOE created the Department of School Culture and Partnerships. This presentation draws on two years of countywide data and 129 student interviews to answer a central question: what actually makes a school feel safe?

What does school safety actually look like to students?

The first wave of school safety — cameras, single-entry points, vape sensors, active-shooter drills — was a necessary and important response to real threats. We've built that infrastructure. Now students are telling us what comes next.

"This school is amazing because you actually get to be yourself and it feels good to be here."

— Middle school student

— Elementary student

What the Research Tells Us
From the Research · Meta-Analytic Findings

Schools have direct leverage on three things that build belonging

❤ Caring & Warmth r = .39

Students who feel genuinely cared for by adults at school show stronger belonging, greater motivation, and better social-emotional outcomes. The quality of adult–student relationships is the single most consistently predictive factor across studies.

What it looks like
  • Teachers checking in personally, not just academically
  • Adults knowing students by name and noticing absence
  • Consistent, warm greetings and transitions
⚖ Fairness r = .40

Students' perception that they are treated equitably — regardless of race, gender, or social status — is strongly linked to belonging and behavioral engagement. Perceived unfairness is one of the fastest trust-destroyers in school settings.

What it looks like
  • Consistent discipline applied the same way for all students
  • Adults responding to bias incidents visibly and promptly
  • Rules that students perceive as reasonable and transparent
◎ Guided Autonomy r = .44

Teachers help students understand and become independent learners — through scaffolded instruction, high expectations, real-world application, and meaningful voice. Structure and agency work together; the combination is what builds belonging.

What it looks like
  • Teachers explaining things until students understand
  • Assignments that develop thinking, not just recall
  • Student voice that genuinely shapes decisions

Korpershoek et al. (2020). The relationships between school belonging and students' motivational, social-emotional, behavioural, and academic outcomes in secondary education. Research Papers in Education, 35(6), 641–680.

Key Question 3
Key Question 3

What do high-belonging schools actually do?

SCOE identified 11 schools scoring in the top tier on both Belonging and Safety. Staff interviewed 129 students with one question: What does this school do that makes you feel like you belong here?

"My teacher makes sure to check in with me once in a while, which makes me know that she sees me and cares about me."

— Elementary student

"Students are able to make a change in the way the school is being run; students have a good voice here."

— High school student
What every high-belonging school had

Five structures students pointed to

  • 1

    Sustained relationships — advisory periods, looping, morning meetings, recurring check-ins with the same adult

  • 2

    Meaningful student voice — embedded in how decisions are actually made, not just an annual survey

  • 3

    Identity reflected in daily life — curriculum, events, and classroom practices that mirror student backgrounds

  • 4

    Clear norms of kindness — peer cultures where kindness is expected and cruelty is socially discouraged

  • 5

    A reporting system students believe in — students know where to go and trust that adults will respond

Key Question 4
Key Question 4

What quietly erodes belonging — even in strong schools?

Schools often focus on adding belonging programs while overlooking routines that damage trust daily. Students described trust as something built gradually and destroyed quickly.

"It's not fair that the whole class is held accountable for one student's misbehavior."

— Elementary student

"I feel really sad sometimes because I have to go somewhere by myself so no one worries about me."

— Elementary student
What erodes trust fast

Four practices students flagged most often

  • 1

    Whole-class consequences — punishing the group for one student's behavior was the most commonly cited trust-damaging practice

  • 2

    Inconsistent discipline — students notice when race, gender, or social standing appears to change how adults respond to the same behavior

  • 3

    Unaddressed bias — when adults hear a slur and don't intervene, students read the silence as endorsement

  • 4

    Allowed isolation — lunch, recess, and passing periods are the highest-risk moments for students without an established peer group

The leadership challenge

The challenge now is to lead with the same urgency around connection that we have brought to physical security.

The next wave of school safety may depend less on what we install in buildings and more on what we build between people.
