Curriculum Mapping for Integration

Build a shared understanding of each course by mapping what students should know, understand, and be able to do.

Stats

Suggested Time
35–50 minutes
Phase
Connect
Group Size
Teacher teams, pathway teams, or interdisciplinary teams
Materials
Course/unit calendars; standards or learning outcomes; sticky notes; markers; whiteboard, chart paper, table, or shared digital space
The Arc
MapTeachWonder

This activity helps teacher teams understand one another's courses before trying to force connections. Teams create a shared map or matrix of what students are expected to know, understand, and be able to do in each class. The emphasis is on empathy: teachers teach one another the logic, goals, timing, and learning demands of their courses.

The map becomes a foundation for future integration work. Teams may eventually use it to identify shared skills, shared concepts, or project opportunities, but the first goal is simpler: make each course visible to the team so teachers can see the student experience more clearly.

COSI Lens · How Deep Are We Preparing to Map?

Name the depth your team is aiming for — it shapes what you pay attention to.

What does each course already connect to — other classes, careers, pathways, or the real world?

Shared IngredientsOverlapping

What skills, concepts, language, or practices show up in more than one course?

One RecipeShared

What course goals might eventually support one shared task, question, product, or project?

How might courses, projects, partners, and outcomes connect over time?

Steps

01

Bring in your COSI target. Name the level your team is aiming for: Seasoning, Shared Ingredients, One Recipe, or Full Menu. This target should shape what you pay attention to, not force connections too early.

02

Choose the mapping format. Decide how your team wants to set up the shared map — a wall matrix, a table, chart paper, or a digital board. Set it up so each course is visible and easy to compare.

03

Create the course matrix. Add each course or teacher to the map, then create two clear spaces for each course: (1) Know + Understand and (2) Be Able to Do. Teams may also add units, time periods, or major learning moments if helpful.

04

Itemize what students need to know and understand. Look for the nouns in your standards — core ideas, topics, texts, concepts, and understandings. Put each major idea on its own sticky note.

05

Itemize what students need to be able to do. Look for the verbs in your standards — actions, processes, skills, practices, and dispositions. Put each major skill or practice on its own sticky note.

06

Place the notes on the matrix. Add the knowledge/concept notes and skill/practice notes to the correct course, unit, or time period. Keep the map visible so the whole team can see the shape of each course.

07

Teach your course to the team. Take turns giving a brief tour of your course map. Explain what students are learning, why it matters, where the work gets challenging, and what students should be able to do by the end.

08

Ask clarifying questions. After each course tour, teammates ask questions to better understand the course. Stay in curiosity. Do not solve, design, or make connections yet — focus on understanding the learning goals and student experience.

09

Capture wonderings. As teammates listen, write wonderings on a separate color sticky note or side section of the map. Use language like: I wonder if…, I noticed…, or This might connect later to… These are possible openings, not commitments.

10

Close with empathy and insight. Each teacher names one thing they now understand better about a teammate's course and one possible connection or question worth revisiting later.

Clarifying Questions + Wonderings

Prompts to keep the conversation in curiosity.

To understand the course

What do students need to understand most deeply? What does success look like in this course? Where do students usually struggle?

To understand the student experience

What do students find meaningful, confusing, repetitive, or challenging? What do you wish students carried with them from other classes?

To surface possible openings

What concepts, skills, or real-world contexts seem like they might connect later? What are we curious to explore next?

Team Deliverable

A shared course matrix that names what students need to know/understand and be able to do in each class, a clearer understanding of one another's courses, and a set of clarifying questions or wonderings that may lead to future integration opportunities.
